General Questions

Understand your model deeper

How to compare the models?

Every time you run a training session, the results are saved in the History Leaderboard. You can compare models based on the metrics (\( R^2 \), RMSE, MAE), residual statistics, and the number of active terms. To test a specific model, go to the Test or Predict tabs and select the Model ID from the dropdown.

What's the difference between the metrics shown in the Training History table and the metrics shown in the Test tab?

Training/Validation metrics (\( R^2 \), RMSE, MAE) are computed on the derivative space (\( dX/dt \)) — this is what SINDy's STLSQ optimizer directly fits. Test tab metrics are computed on the state space (\( x(t) \)), after forward-simulating the discovered equations with solve_ivp. These two sets of numbers use different units and are not directly comparable. A model can have excellent Training RMSE(\( dX \)) and still show poor Test RMSE(\( x \)) if the small derivative-space errors compound during simulation. Always check both.

Why is R² high but the simulated trajectory still diverges from the true data?

\( R^2 \) shown in the Training tab is computed on the derivative space (\( dX/dt \)), which is what SINDy's loss function actually optimizes — not on the simulated trajectory \( x(t) \). A model can fit the derivative field almost perfectly and still diverge when numerically integrated, because small coefficient errors compound over time during simulation. This is exactly why the Test tab exists: it forward-integrates your equations with solve_ivp and reports RMSE/\( R^2 \) on \( x(t) \) directly, which is the metric that actually matters for prediction.

What does "Sparsity parameter is too big and eliminated all coefficients" mean?

Your Sparsity Threshold is set higher than the magnitude of every coefficient in the true (or best-fit) equations, so STLSQ pruned everything to zero — the model effectively found \( dX/dt = 0 \) for your system. Lower the threshold (try the "type exact threshold" box for fine control) and re-train.

Why do Train R² and Validation R² both look reasonably good, but the equations found still contain extra "junk" terms that shouldn't be there?

With Random Sampling split, individual data points are shuffled before splitting. Because the trajectory changes smoothly over time, a validation point can sit just one or two time-steps away from a training point — making them nearly identical (high autocorrelation). The model effectively "sees" a near-duplicate of each validation point during training, which can make the validation score look better than it should, without actually confirming the equations are correct. Random Block split (chopping the trajectory into contiguous chunks before shuffling) reduces this leak while still covering the full trajectory — consider it if your Random Sampling results look suspiciously clean but the equations still have unexpected terms.

For damped/decaying system, why did Time-based split find cleaner equations than Random Sampling or Random Block, even though its Validation R² was lower (or even negative)?

Systems whose amplitude decays over time (e.g. a damped Duffing oscillator) concentrate most of their useful information — the signal needed to correctly identify nonlinear terms — in the early, high-amplitude portion of the trajectory. Time-based split puts that entire high-amplitude region into training, giving SINDy the strongest possible signal to correctly identify terms like \( x^3 \). Random splits mix in the low-amplitude "tail" (where the signal-to-noise ratio is poor), which can introduce spurious cross-terms. The low or negative Validation R² for Time-based split in this case is a separate, expected artifact: the validation set (the decayed tail) has very low true-signal variance, so even a small absolute error produces a poor R² — it does not mean the equations are wrong. Lesson: a lower Validation R² does not automatically mean worse equations — always inspect the actual discovered equations, not just the metric.

My system has an explicit time-dependent term (e.g. dx/dt = -x + sin(t)) — why can't SINDy find it directly?

SINDy assumes the system is autonomous: \( dx/dt = f(x) \), depending only on the current state, never explicitly on time t. A term like sin(t) cannot appear in the candidate library because t is not a state variable. Workaround: add an auxiliary state \(τ\) that tracks time \((dτ/dt = 1)\) to your data, so the system becomes autonomous in the augmented state \((x, τ)\). SINDy can then discover \(sin(τ)\) as a valid term, since \(τ\) is now a regular state variable. This has been verified to work in this module (see the Fourier test datasets).

Should I trust a model just because every metric looks good?

Be appropriately skeptical. Check: (1) Training and Validation metrics are close to each other (not overfitting), (2) Residual Diagnostics show no structured pattern (flat residual-vs-time, no FFT peak, tight scatter along the y=x line), (3) the discovered equations have few, simple terms with coefficients that make physical sense, and (4) the Test tab confirms the equations generalize to a genuinely new initial condition. No single metric is sufficient on its own.

Why might solve_ivp fail or diverge on the test trajectory, even though the same discovered equations fit the training data well?

SINDy only fits your equations within the region of state space actually explored by the training trajectory. If your test file's initial condition sends the trajectory into state values well outside that explored region, the discovered terms — especially higher-degree polynomial ones — can extrapolate very poorly there. A polynomial coefficient that looks perfectly reasonable near \(x≈1\) can produce enormous derivative values at \(x≈10\), since polynomial growth is unbounded. This is why choosing a test initial condition that is meaningfully different but not wildly outside the training range gives the most informative test.

Why is the prediction diverging after some time?

SINDy models are sensitive to the Prediction Horizon. If the identified dynamics are slightly unstable or if the data was noisy, errors can accumulate during integration. Try increasing the Sparsity Threshold to find a more robust physical representation.
